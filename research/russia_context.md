# Russian Tech and Cultural Context (2000s-2010s)

## Historical Background

### Post-Soviet Transition (1991-2000)
- Collapse of Soviet Union in 1991
- Economic chaos and "wild 90s"
- Rise of oligarchs and new business class
- Emergence of internet infrastructure
- Tech sector largely underdeveloped

### Putin Era Beginning (2000-2008)
- Vladimir Putin becomes president in 2000
- Stabilization of economy via oil/gas revenues
- Consolidation of state power
- Growing middle class with internet access
- Increasing state control over media

## Russian Internet Culture (Runet)

### Early Internet (1990s-2000s)
- Limited access, primarily in major cities
- Dominated by tech enthusiasts and students
- Forums, chat rooms, file-sharing networks
- Piracy culture (music, movies, software)
- Less commercial than Western internet

### Web 2.0 Era (2005-2012)
- Broadband expansion to homes
- Rise of social networks and blogs
- LiveJournal (ЖЖ) as blogging platform
- Odnoklassniki for reconnecting old friends
- Growing importance of online communication

### Unique Characteristics
- **Language barrier**: Cyrillic alphabet creates semi-isolated ecosystem
- **Local platforms**: Yandex (search), Mail.ru (portal), VKontakte (social)
- **Piracy acceptance**: Relaxed attitudes toward copyright
- **Community focus**: Strong emphasis on groups and forums
- **Political discussion**: Internet as space for opposition views

## Russian Tech Entrepreneurship

### Challenges
- Lack of venture capital infrastructure
- Legal uncertainty and corruption
- Brain drain (emigration of talent)
- Government interference
- Weak intellectual property protection

### Success Stories
- **Yandex**: Russian Google, founded 1997
- **Kaspersky**: Cybersecurity, founded 1997
- **Mail.Ru**: Portal and services, founded 1998
- **VKontakte**: Social network, founded 2006
- **Telegram**: Messaging app, founded 2013

### Investment Landscape
- Yuri Milner's DST Global (Mail.Ru Group)
- Russian oligarchs investing in tech
- Limited Western VC involvement
- "Silicon Valley" aspirations vs. reality

## University Culture

### St. Petersburg State University (SPbGU)
- One of Russia's oldest and most prestigious universities
- Founded by Peter the Great in 1724
- Strong mathematics and science programs
- Historical intellectual center
- Both Durov brothers studied here

### Student Life & Tech Culture
- Programming competitions and olympiads
- Student initiatives and projects
- Mix of academic rigidity and creative freedom
- International connections
- Breeding ground for tech talent

## Business Environment (2006-2012)

### Opportunities
- Rapidly growing internet user base
- Untapped markets in social networking
- Young, tech-savvy population
- High-quality technical education
- Low initial competition

### Risks
- Political unpredictability
- Pressure for government cooperation
- Raider attacks (hostile takeovers)
- Corruption and bureaucracy
- Capital flight concerns

### Government Relations
- Increasing demands for user data
- Pressure for content censorship
- National security concerns
- Balance between growth and control
- Post-2012: Tightening grip

## Social & Political Context

### Russian Society (2006-2012)
- Growing prosperity from oil boom
- Expanding middle class
- Increasing internet penetration
- Youth culture embracing technology
- Growing gap between generations

### Political Climate
- Medvedev presidency (2008-2012): Relative liberalization
- Putin return (2012): Tightening control
- 2011-2012 protests: Opposition movements
- Use of social media for organization
- Government concern over online activism

### Freedom & Censorship
- Traditional media largely state-controlled
- Internet as relatively free space (until ~2012)
- Growing government attention to online content
- Tension between freedom and control
- Post-2012: Increased restrictions

## Cultural Values & Tech

### Russian Programmer Culture
- Strong mathematical foundation
- Pride in elegant solutions
- Competitive spirit (olympiads, contests)
- International ambitions
- Mix of idealism and pragmatism

### Social Attitudes
- Collectivist traditions vs. individual freedom
- Distrust of authorities
- Value of personal connections (связи - svyazi)
- Tolerance of informal/gray areas
- Humor and irony as coping mechanisms

## Language & Terminology

### Russian Internet Slang
- Borrowed English terms (often modified)
- Unique Russian expressions
- Gaming and forum culture influence
- Generational markers
- Constantly evolving

### Key Terms
- **Ботаник** (botanik): Geek/nerd
- **Гопник** (gopnik): Street hoodlum
- **Тусовка** (tusovka): Social scene/community
- **Хакер** (hacker): Programmer/hacker (broader meaning)
- **Френд** (friend): Social media friend

## Media & Publishing

### Tech Media
- Limited specialized tech journalism
- Business press (Коммерсантъ, Ведомости)
- Blogs as primary source
- Hagibu.ru and other tech blogs
- International coverage (TechCrunch, etc.)

### Book Publishing
- Growing interest in tech biographies
- "Код Дурова" as rare Russian tech book
- Translation of Western business books
- Limited domestic entrepreneurship literature

## Comparison: Russia vs. Silicon Valley

### Similarities
- Young, ambitious founders
- Technical excellence
- Rapid growth potential
- Disruption of traditional industries
- Global ambitions

### Differences
- Political interference
- Weaker legal framework
- Less developed VC ecosystem
- Geographic isolation (less networking)
- Different exit options

## 2012-2014 Turning Point

### Shift in Climate
- Putin's return to presidency (2012)
- Increased pressure on internet freedom
- Laws targeting online content
- VKontakte conflicts as symptom
- Beginning of "digital sovereignty" push

### Impact on Tech Sector
- Some entrepreneurs leaving Russia
- Self-censorship increasing
- Foreign investment concerns
- Brain drain acceleration
- Durov's 2014 exit as symbolic moment
