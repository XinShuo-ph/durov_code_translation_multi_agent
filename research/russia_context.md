# Russian Cultural and Tech Context - Research Notes

## Historical Context (1984-2013)

### Soviet Era Legacy (1984-1991)
- **Durov's Birth (1984)**: Late Soviet period, pre-Perestroika
- **Education System**: Strong emphasis on mathematics, science
- **Intellectual Culture**: Academic families highly respected
- **Philology Tradition**: Classical education valued (Durov's father's field)
- **Technology**: Limited computer access, mostly academic institutions

### Collapse & Transition (1991-1999)
- **Soviet Collapse (1991)**: Durov age 7
- **Economic Crisis**: "Wild 90s" - chaos, oligarchs rising
- **St. Petersburg**: Significant political center (Putin connection)
- **Youth Experience**: Generation grew up during extreme instability
- **Digital Divide**: Computers becoming more accessible

### Putin Era & Stability (2000-2013)
- **Economic Recovery**: Oil/gas revenues, growing middle class
- **Internet Expansion**: Broadband becoming common
- **Tech Scene Emergence**: First generation of Russian internet companies
- **Control & Freedom**: Balance between economic freedom and political control

## St. Petersburg Geography & Culture

### City Character
- **Former Capital**: Imperial history, cultural prestige
- **Architecture**: Historic center vs. Soviet panel housing
- **Intellectuals**: Strong literary, academic tradition
- **Youth Culture**: More European-influenced than Moscow

### Panel Housing (Панельные дома)
- **Description**: Prefabricated concrete apartment blocks
- **Era**: Built 1960s-1980s, Soviet mass housing solution
- **Characteristics**:
  - Uniform appearance
  - 5-16 story buildings
  - Small apartments (40-60 sq meters typical)
  - Communal yards between buildings
- **Social Meaning**: Working/middle class housing, not prestigious
- **Book Context**: Durov's childhood neighborhood (Chapter 1)

### Nevsky Prospekt
- **Main Street**: St. Petersburg's central avenue
- **Historic**: Imperial-era buildings, cultural landmarks
- **Singer Building**: VK office location, Art Nouveau landmark
- **Symbolism**: Heart of city, commerce, culture

### Neighborhood Terms from Chapter 1
- **Подъезд (pod'yezd)**: Building entrance, stairwell
- **Двор (dvor)**: Courtyard between buildings
- **Стройка (stroyka)**: Construction site
- **Гопники (gopniki)**: Street toughs, working-class youth subculture

## Russian Internet Culture (Runet)

### Early Development (1990s-2000s)
- **Runet**: Russian-language internet
- **Forums**: Huge forum culture (phpBB, vBulletin)
- **Piracy**: Rampant, accepted cultural norm
- **Anonymity**: Valued more than Western internet
- **Creativity**: Rich meme culture, humor

### Social Networks Before VK
- **LiveJournal (Zhivoy Zhurnal)**: Blogging platform, very popular
- **Odnoklassniki**: "Classmates" - older demographic
- **Moy Mir**: Mail.ru's failed attempt
- **Russian Facebook**: Never succeeded in Russia

### Distinctive Features of Runet
1. **Cyrillic**: Language barrier from English internet
2. **Piracy**: MP3s, movies freely shared (legally gray)
3. **Anonymity**: Pseudonyms common
4. **Memes**: Unique Russian meme culture
5. **Forums**: Strong community discussion culture
6. **Politics**: Mix of freedom and self-censorship

## Russian Tech/Startup Scene (2006-2013)

### Major Players
- **Yandex**: Russian search engine (Google competitor)
- **Mail.ru Group**: Portal, email, games
- **Rambler**: Portal and media
- **Kaspersky**: Antivirus, global success
- **VKontakte**: Social network
- **Ozon**: E-commerce (Amazon-like)

### Characteristics
- **Moscow-centric**: Mostly Moscow, some St. Petersburg
- **Less VC money**: Compared to Silicon Valley
- **Oligarch involvement**: Business tycoons investing
- **Government relations**: Complex, necessary
- **Brain drain**: Best programmers often leave for US

### Programmer Culture
- **Excellence**: Strong mathematical/CS education
- **Competitive**: Programming competitions (ACM, etc.)
- **Undervalued**: Lower salaries than West
- **Entrepreneurial**: New opportunities post-Soviet
- **Community**: Strong developer communities (Habr.com)

## Business Culture & Practices

### Negotiation Style
- **Direct**: Can be very blunt
- **Hierarchical**: Respect for authority/age
- **Personal**: Relationships matter greatly
- **Drinking**: Business often done over drinks
- **Informal**: Less written contracts than West
- **Trust Issues**: Soviet legacy of distrust

### Corporate Governance
- **Weak Rule of Law**: Contracts less enforceable
- **Connections (Blat)**: Who you know matters
- **Raiding**: Hostile takeovers, legal manipulation
- **State Involvement**: Government can intervene
- **Oligarch System**: Extreme wealth concentration

### Durov's Unconventional Style
- Rejects traditional Russian business culture
- More Silicon Valley-influenced
- Confronts authority directly
- Values principles over profits (unusual)
- Public, not behind-closed-doors

## Language & Slang

### Internet Slang (Интернет-жаргон)
- **Школота (shkolota)**: School kids (derogatory)
- **Ботаник (botanik)**: Nerd/geek (from "botany")
- **Тян (tyan)**: Girl (from Japanese ちゃん)
- **Кун (kun)**: Guy (from Japanese くん)
- **Няша (nyasha)**: Cute (from Japanese にゃー)
- **Мимими**: Expression of cuteness
- **Олдфаг (oldfag)**: Old-timer on internet

### Tech Terms (Borrowed)
- **Хакер (haker)**: Hacker
- **Юзер (yuser)**: User
- **Баг (bag)**: Bug
- **Фича (ficha)**: Feature
- **Коммитить (kommitit')**: To commit (git)

### Cultural Terms
- **Гопник (gopnik)**: Working-class street youth subculture
  - Characteristics: Squatting, tracksuits, aggressive
  - Social class marker
  - Featured in Chapter 1 (Durov avoiding them)
- **Стиляги (stilyagi)**: Style-conscious youth (historical)

## Social Stratification

### Post-Soviet Class System
1. **Oligarchs**: Billionaires, extreme wealth
2. **New Rich**: Entrepreneurs, successful professionals
3. **Middle Class**: Growing, urban, educated
4. **Working Class**: Industrial workers, service sector
5. **Poor**: Rural, elderly, marginalized

### Education & Class
- **Academic Families**: High prestige, moderate income (Durov family)
- **University**: Still meritocratic path to success
- **Technical Education**: Programmers gaining status/wealth
- **Classical Education**: Philology, philosophy valued

## Content Regulation & Censorship

### Legal Framework (as of 2013)
- **Extremism Laws**: Broad, used to block content
- **Copyright**: Weakly enforced domestically
- **Privacy Laws**: Evolving
- **Internet Freedom**: Relatively open (changing)

### Government Pressure Tactics
- **Informal Requests**: Phone calls, meetings
- **Legal Threats**: Selective prosecution
- **Economic Pressure**: Through investors
- **Media Campaigns**: Negative coverage
- **License Threats**: Threatening business licenses

### Durov's Resistance
- Publicly refused data requests
- Protected opposition figures' pages
- Confrontational with authorities
- Eventually led to his ouster (2014)

## Economic Context

### 2006-2013 Period
- **Oil Boom**: High commodity prices fueling growth
- **Middle Class Growth**: Consumer market expanding
- **Internet Penetration**: Growing rapidly
- **Investment Climate**: Improving but risky
- **Brain Drain**: Talented programmers emigrating

### Tech Funding
- **Less VC**: Compared to Silicon Valley
- **Oligarch Money**: Business tycoons as investors
- **Mail.ru Group**: Major tech investor
- **DST/Mail.ru**: Yuri Milner's fund active

## Generation Gap

### Durov's Generation (born ~1984)
- **Last Soviet Generation**: Remember USSR collapse
- **Digital Natives**: First to grow up with internet
- **Globalized**: More Western-influenced than parents
- **Entrepreneurial**: New opportunities available
- **Less Soviet Baggage**: Different worldview than parents

### Older Generation
- **Soviet Nostalgia**: Remember stability
- **Suspicious of Tech**: Don't understand social networks
- **Traditional Values**: More conservative
- **Odnoklassniki Users**: Prefer different social network
