# VKontakte (VK) - Company History

## Basic Information
- **Name**: ВКонтакте (VKontakte) / VK
- **Translation**: "In Contact" or "In Touch"
- **Founded**: October 10, 2006
- **Founder**: Pavel Durov
- **Headquarters**: Singer House (Дом Зингера), Nevsky Prospekt, St. Petersburg, Russia
- **Type**: Social Networking Service

## Founding Story

### The Beginning (2006)
- Founded on Pavel Durov's 22nd birthday (October 10, 2006)
- Initially created as a social network for Russian university students
- Inspired by Facebook (which was still limited to US colleges at the time)
- Technical architecture designed by Nikolai Durov (Pavel's brother)

### Singer House Headquarters
- Located at Nevsky Prospekt 28, St. Petersburg
- Historic building built in 1902-1904 for Singer sewing machine company
- Distinctive copper globe on the dome
- 6th floor became VKontakte offices
- Iconic location overlooking Nevsky Prospekt and Kazan Cathedral

## Growth Timeline

### 2006-2007: Launch and Early Growth
- Launched as university-focused social network
- Rapid adoption among Russian students
- Word-of-mouth growth
- Open registration (unlike Facebook's initial college-only model)

### 2008-2010: Explosive Growth
- Became largest social network in Russia and CIS
- Expanded features: photos, music, video, groups
- Reached millions of users
- Developed reputation for hosting pirated content (music, movies)

### 2010-2012: Peak VKontakte Era (Book Period)
- By 2012: 100+ million registered users
- 20+ million daily active users (mentioned in book)
- Team of ~30 core programmers managing complex infrastructure
- Revenue from virtual gifts, advertising, virtual currency ("голоса" - voices)

### 2012-2014: Investor Conflicts
- Battles with Mail.ru Group and other investors
- Pressure to monetize more aggressively
- Government pressure over content moderation
- Durov's resistance to censorship

### 2014: Durov's Departure
- April 2014: Durov announced he was "dismissed" as CEO
- Sold his remaining shares
- Left Russia
- Company fully controlled by Mail.ru Group (now VK Company)

## Key Features

### Social Networking
- User profiles with photos, status updates
- Friends lists
- Wall posts and comments
- Private messaging

### Media Sharing
- Photo albums
- Music streaming (became controversial for piracy)
- Video hosting
- Document sharing

### Groups and Communities
- Public pages (паблики)
- Private groups
- Community management tools
- Major platform for Russian internet culture

### Virtual Economy
- Virtual gifts
- In-app currency ("голоса" - voices)
- Games platform
- Payment system

## Technical Architecture

### Infrastructure
- Custom-built from scratch (not using standard frameworks)
- Highly optimized for Russian user patterns
- Massive scale handling
- Data centers in Russia

### Team
- Small, elite programming team (~30 people for core development)
- Flat hierarchy
- Meritocratic culture
- High technical standards

### Technology Stack
- C++ for core components
- Custom database solutions
- Proprietary protocols

## Corporate Culture (as described in book)

### "Nerd Kingdom"
- Programmer-centric culture
- Casual dress code (t-shirts, jeans)
- Flat hierarchy
- Creative freedom
- Long working hours by choice

### Office Environment
- Singer House 6th floor
- Quirky room names: "Сквот ИЕ" (IE Squat), "Наркомпросвет" (Narkomprosvet), "Злые пользователи" (Angry Users), "Музей Дурова" (Durov Museum)
- Orange juice machines
- Casual atmosphere

### Hiring Philosophy
- Technical excellence paramount
- Cultural fit important
- Programmers as "first-class citizens"
- Other roles (sales, marketing) considered secondary

## Business Model

### Revenue Streams
1. **Virtual Gifts**: Users purchase and send digital gifts
2. **Virtual Currency**: "Голоса" (Voices) for in-app purchases
3. **Advertising**: Display ads, targeted advertising
4. **Games Platform**: Revenue share from third-party games
5. **Premium Features**: Special stickers, customizations

### Investor Relations
- **Vyacheslav Mirilashvili**: Early investor
- **Mail.ru Group**: Major investor, eventually gained control
- Contentious relationship with investors over company direction
- Durov resisted aggressive monetization

## Controversies

### Piracy
- Massive amounts of pirated music and video
- Legal pressure from rights holders
- VK's approach: reactive rather than proactive removal
- Major attraction for users (free content)

### Government Relations
- Requests for user data from authorities
- Pressure to censor opposition content
- 2011-2012: Refused to block Navalny's group
- Maintained independence despite pressure

### Privacy
- Collected extensive user data
- Questions about data security
- Russian authorities' access to data

## Competition

### In Russia
- **Odnoklassniki**: Older demographic, classmates reunification
- **Facebook**: Growing but never overtook VK in Russia
- **Twitter**: Different use case
- **Instagram**: Photo sharing competition

### Global Context
- Often called "Russian Facebook"
- More features than Facebook (music, video hosting)
- Better localized for Russian users
- Faster development cycle

## Key People (VKontakte Era)

## Cultural Impact

### On Russian Internet
- Defined Russian social media landscape
- Created Russian internet slang and memes
- Platform for youth culture
- Political organizing (protests)

### On Music Industry
- Disrupted traditional music distribution
- Free access to vast music libraries
- Changed how Russians consume music

### On Media
- User-generated content explosion
- Citizen journalism
- Viral content distribution

## Post-Durov Era (Context)

### 2014-Present
- Merged with Mail.ru Group
- Rebranded as "VK" internationally
- Removed much pirated content
- Added more monetization
- Integrated with government services
- Became more corporate
