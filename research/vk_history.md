# VKontakte (VK) History

## Overview
- **Full Name**: ВКонтакте (VKontakte) - "In Contact" in English
- **Current Name**: VK (rebranded 2021)
- **Founded**: October 10, 2006
- **Founder**: Pavel Durov
- **Headquarters**: St. Petersburg, Russia (Singer House initially)
- **Type**: Social networking service

---

## Pre-Launch (2005-2006)

### Background
- Pavel Durov ran student forum spbgu.ru at St. Petersburg State University
- Facebook launched in 2004, growing rapidly in US universities
- Russian internet market lacked dominant social network
- Odnoklassniki launched in March 2006 (focused on reconnecting old classmates)

### Conception
- Durov noticed Facebook's success model
- Wanted to create Russian equivalent with better features
- Initially called the project "StudZona" (Student Zone)
- Recruited brother Nikolai for technical architecture

---

## Founding and Early Years (2006-2008)

### Launch (October 10, 2006)
- Launched on Durov's 22nd birthday
- Initially registration-only (required university email)
- First users: St. Petersburg university students
- Domain: vkontakte.ru

### Key Features at Launch
- Personal profiles with photos
- Friend connections
- Wall for posts
- Private messaging
- Groups and communities

### Rapid Growth (2007)
- Opened to general public
- Added music and video sharing (key differentiator)
- 3 million users by end of 2007
- First investment from Yuri Milner's DST (Digital Sky Technologies)

### Mirilashvili Investment (2007)
- Vyacheslav Mirilashvili invested for 10% stake
- Lev Leviev also invested for 10% stake
- Provided early capital for growth
- Total investment reportedly around $1 million

---

## Growth Phase (2008-2011)

### User Milestones
- **2008**: 20 million users
- **2009**: 50 million users
- **2010**: 100 million users
- **2011**: 135 million users

### Feature Development
- **Audio**: Massive library of music (mostly pirated)
- **Video**: YouTube-like video hosting
- **Games**: Social gaming platform
- **API**: Opened platform for third-party developers
- **VK Coins**: Virtual currency for gifts and games

### Cultural Impact
- Became dominant platform for Russian youth
- Known for tolerance of pirated content
- Enabled music discovery and sharing
- Communities (паблики) became influential

### Singer House Headquarters
- VK offices on 6th floor of historic Singer House
- Located on Nevsky Prospekt, St. Petersburg
- Iconic location: former Singer sewing machine company building
- Became symbol of VK's identity

---

## Investor Conflicts (2011-2014)

### Mail.ru Group Entry
- 2010: Mail.ru Group began acquiring VK stakes
- Bought shares from Mirilashvili and Leviev
- 2011: Acquired DST's stake in VK
- 2013: Controlled ~52% of shares

### United Capital Partners (UCP)
- Acquired remaining shares from Mirilashvili/Leviev (2013)
- Combined with Mail.ru's stake gave effective control
- Pavel Durov reduced to minority shareholder

### Tensions with Durov
- Durov resisted shareholder control
- Refused to hand over user data to authorities
- Conflict over company direction
- Shareholders sought to monetize and moderate content

---

## Political Pressure (2011-2014)

### Government Relations
- 2011: Protests following parliamentary elections
- VK groups used for protest organization
- Durov refused to shut down opposition groups
- FSB reportedly requested user data access

### Famous Incidents
- **December 2011**: Durov posted middle finger to FSB requests
- **2012**: Defied orders to remove protest content
- **2013**: Publicly criticized government censorship

### Legal Issues
- Copyright lawsuits from Western music labels
- Pressure to remove pirated content
- GEMA (German rights organization) lawsuit

---

## Durov's Departure (2014)

### Timeline
- **January 2014**: Durov fired from CEO position
- **February 2014**: Reinstatement after public outcry
- **April 2014**: Durov announced resignation
- **April 21, 2014**: Officially resigned
- Sold 12% stake to Ivan Tavrin (MegaFon)

### Durov's Statement
- Cited pressure to provide user data
- Said he was asked to give up group information
- Stated Russia "incompatible with Internet business"
- Left Russia, reported to not have returned

---

## Post-Durov Era (2014-Present)

### New Leadership
- **Boris Dobrodeyev** became CEO (son of state TV head)
- Company integrated more closely with Mail.ru Group
- 2021: Rebranded to simply "VK"

### Changes Under New Management
- Stricter content moderation
- Copyright compliance efforts
- Integration with other Mail.ru services
- More cooperation with authorities

### Current Status (2024)
- Still largest social network in Russia/CIS
- ~100+ million monthly active users
- Owned by VK Company (formerly Mail.ru Group)
- Part of broader Russian tech ecosystem

## VK vs. Facebook Comparison

| Aspect | VKontakte | Facebook |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Launch | October 2006 | February 2004 |
| Initial Focus | Russian students | Harvard students |
| Music/Video | Embedded, tolerant of piracy | Strict copyright |
| Privacy | Less strict moderation | More policies |
| Market | Russia/CIS dominant | Global dominant |
| Founder exit | 2014 (forced) | Still involved |

---

## Cultural Significance

### In Russia/CIS
- "Staying in contact" became synonymous with VK
- Democratized music access (despite piracy issues)
- Platform for youth subcultures
- Alternative to Western platforms

### Controversial Legacy
- Enabled both free expression and surveillance
- Copyright battles with Western companies
- Platform for opposition AND nationalist groups
- Symbol of Russian internet's unique path
